2. Intro to LLMs for Human Resources

Large Language Models (LLMs) offer HR professionals a powerful tool for automating tasks, generating ideas, and providing insights across various functions. These models can assist in talent acquisition, employee engagement, policy development, and more.

What Are We Talking About?

When you hear terms like "Artificial Intelligence" (AI) and "large language models" (LLMs), think of them as advanced computer tools that can understand and respond to written instructions in everyday language—much like having a conversation. The "large" in LLMs refers to the vast amount of written material these tools have been exposed to, like how a person learns language and information by reading many books and articles.

Unlike software like Microsoft Word or Excel that can only follow specific commands, LLMs can:

  • Understand your requests written in plain English.
  • Create written content that sounds natural and professional.
  • Adjust their responses based on your specific needs.
  • Learn from the examples you provide.
  • Help with many kinds of writing and analysis tasks.

Why Should HR Professionals Care?

As an HR professional, you likely spend much of your day writing, reading, and analyzing information. These new AI tools can help make many daily tasks easier and more efficient. Here’s how:

Writing Assistance:

  • Help draft policies and procedures.
  • Create job descriptions.
  • Develop training materials.
  • Write professional emails and announcements.

Information Analysis:

  • Help understand employee feedback.
  • Summarize survey responses.
  • Spot important trends in workplace information.
  • Suggest solutions to common workplace challenges.

Important Things to Remember

As you start using these tools:

AI Tools Support Your Work, They Don't Replace Your Expertise
  • They help you work better, not work less.
  • They need your professional judgment.
  • They work best when guided by your HR knowledge.
Privacy and Security Come First
  • Never share private employee information. Do not paste PII into public AI tools.
  • Always double-check AI-generated content for accuracy.

Know What AI Can and Can't Do

  • It can make mistakes (see "Hallucinations" in Section 6).
  • It needs guidance to match your organization's style.
  • It cannot make important decisions for you.

Remember, the goal isn't to become an AI expert—it's to learn how to use these tools to make your HR work more effective while maintaining the personal touch that makes HR so important.

3. Choosing the Right AI Tool UPDATED 2025

Selecting the appropriate AI model is crucial. The 2024 guide listed models that are now last-generation. As of late 2025, the field is dominated by highly specialized, multimodal, and efficient models with massive context windows.

AI Assistants & Company Knowledge (Formerly "Custom GPTs")

The concept of "Custom GPTs" has evolved into a more powerful and integrated ecosystem.

  • AI Assistants & "Apps": Instead of just custom-prompted chatbots, platforms now offer "Apps in ChatGPT" or "AI Assistants". These are tools that can be deeply integrated into your chat, allowing the AI to use embedded UI components (like calendars, slides, or project boards) and take action on your behalf.
  • Company Knowledge Connectors: For HR, the most significant change is the ability to connect your AI to your company's own data. Services like ChatGPT Business can now connect directly to your Google Drive, SharePoint, or Slack. This allows the AI to answer questions using your specific company policy manuals, employee handbooks, and internal data, providing cited, accurate answers. This is a commercial, easy-to-use version of the RAG framework (see Section 4).

4. Setting Up Your AI (RAG & Context)

Before diving into specific tasks, setting up the AI to align with your objectives is essential. A well-thought-out setup process helps tailor the AI's output to your unique requirements, whether generating detailed analyses, producing specific document formats, or adhering to particular HR guidelines.

Imagine you're training a new HR team member. Instead of expecting them to know everything, you give them access to a filing cabinet full of your company's HR documents, policies, and procedures to reference.

RAG works similarly—it's a way to let the AI look up and use specific documents you provide when creating responses.

  • Without RAG: The AI uses only its general knowledge (which might be outdated or incorrect).
  • With RAG: The AI can reference your specific documents (like your employee handbook or policies) to provide more accurate, relevant responses.
2025 Update: RAG is Now a Product

While RAG began as a technical concept, it is now a core feature of most enterprise AI platforms. When you use "Company Knowledge" (see Section 3) and connect your Google Drive or SharePoint, you are using a pre-built, user-friendly RAG system.

You can also perform RAG manually by uploading documents (PDFs, .txt, .csv) directly into the chat window of models like Claude 4.5 or Gemini 2.5 and asking questions about them.

A Real-World Example: Updating Parental Leave

Task: You need to update your parental leave policy.

Prompt with RAG (by uploading documents):

[Uploading Current_Policy.pdf and New_State_Law.pdf] Using our current parental leave policy (Current_Policy.pdf), help me update it to be compliant with the new state regulations (New_State_Law.pdf). Ensure the new draft maintains our company's formal tone and terminology.

The AI can now reference your exact policy language, maintain your company's terminology, and ensure consistency, rather than giving generic advice.

Setting the context upfront ensures that the AI tailors its output to align with organizational specifics and legal requirements. Use a "pre-prompt" or system instruction to guide the entire conversation.

Context Example (Setting the Stage)

"For the following tasks, consider the context of a mid-sized technology company in the United States with around 500 employees. Assume a general corporate environment that values diversity, inclusion, and employee development."

Tone Example (Setting the Voice)

"Please use a professional and concise tone in all responses, suitable for communication with HR professionals. Write responses as if addressing senior HR leaders, with formal language, structured guidance, and clarity."

Format Example (Setting the Structure)

"For complex topics, structure responses using headings and bullet points to enhance readability."

6. How to Assess AI Output

Effectively using AI in HR requires not just crafting good prompts, but also the skill to assess and interpret the content generated. AI responds based on patterns, not true understanding, so HR professionals must evaluate each response's relevance, accuracy, and appropriateness.

Understanding AI Mistakes: What Are "Hallucinations"?

Just like humans can misremember facts, AI models can "hallucinate"—they create information that sounds convincing but is false, misleading, or fabricated. This is a critical risk in HR.

For example, an AI might:

  • Create fake policy references that sound official but don't exist (e.g., "As per the 'Workplace Harmony Act of 2023'...").
  • Generate statistics that seem reasonable but aren't based on real data (e.g., "78% of employees in your sector prefer this...").
  • Mix up details from different sources or laws.
Always "Trust but Verify"

Think of AI-generated content like a first draft from a helpful but imperfect assistant. Your expertise and judgment are essential to ensure its correctness.

  1. Fact-Check Critical Information: Cross-check any legal requirements (e.g., FMLA, ADA, Title IX) with official government sources or your legal counsel. Verify policy references against your company's actual policies.
  2. Build Verification Into Your Prompts: Ask the AI to "show its work."
    • "Write a draft remote work policy. Include notes in [brackets] about which sections need legal review. Highlight any areas where you're making assumptions about company practices."
    • "When analyzing this survey data, please note any gaps in the data or assumptions made in your analysis."
  3. Check for Bias: Carefully review responses for implicit or explicit biases, particularly in sensitive areas like hiring, performance management, or employee relations. Ensure responses align with your DEI principles.

Creating a Verification Routine

Develop a standard checklist for reviewing AI-generated content:

Category Question to Ask
Legal Compliance Does this comply with current laws? Does it need legal team review?
Company Alignment Does this align with our policies? Is the tone in harmony with our company culture?
Practical Implementation Is this realistic for our organization? Have we considered all stakeholders?
Data Accuracy Are statistics current and verified? Are all numbers correct?

7. AI Ethics & Safety for HR

Leveraging AI in human resources has transformative potential but also introduces significant ethical and practical challenges. HR professionals must balance efficiency with responsibility, ensuring that AI-driven outputs align with organizational values, protect employee data, and promote equity.

Data Privacy is Paramount

HR functions involve highly sensitive employee data. Protecting this data is your number one priority.

  • Never include Personally Identifiable Information (PII) in public AI tools. This includes names, Social Security numbers, home addresses, or specific performance details.
  • Use anonymized examples (e.g., "Draft a warning letter for 'an employee' who has 'missed multiple deadlines'").
  • For sensitive work, use secure, enterprise-grade AI platforms that have clear data privacy agreements, or use self-hosted open-source models (see Section 3).

AI models are trained on vast datasets from the internet, which contain human societal biases. These biases can inadvertently appear in AI-generated content.

  • Scrutinize Hiring Content: When using AI to draft job descriptions or interview questions, check for language that could subtly discourage or favor a particular gender, race, age, or background.
  • Audit AI-Assisted Decisions: If using AI to screen resumes or analyze performance data, ensure the criteria are objective and job-related. Regularly audit the outcomes to ensure the AI is not creating a disparate impact on any protected group.
  • Use Bias-Checking Prompts:
    "Review this job description for any language that could be perceived as biased or non-inclusive. Suggest 3 revisions to make it more welcoming to all qualified candidates."

Building employee trust requires clear communication about how AI is used in HR processes.

  • Inform Employees: Be transparent about where and how AI is being used, such as in crafting policies, analyzing engagement surveys, or generating training materials.
  • Explain AI-Driven Decisions: If AI contributes to a decision (e.g., identifying employees for a development program), ensure the rationale behind the decision is clear, understandable, and explainable.

AI is a tool, not a decision-maker. Define the scope of its use to prevent over-reliance.

  • Limit to Low/Medium-Stakes Tasks: Use AI primarily for drafting, brainstorming, and initial analysis.
  • Humans Make High-Stakes Decisions: The final decision in any sensitive employee relations issue, hiring, promotion, or termination must be made by an experienced HR professional and/or manager.
  • Define Roles: An AI can draft a policy, but experienced HR professionals and legal advisors must perform the final review and approval.

8. Glossary & Resources UPDATED 2025

AI (Artificial Intelligence)
A type of computer technology that can perform tasks that typically require human thinking. In HR, it's like having a smart assistant that can help with writing, analysis, and routine tasks.
Bias in AI
When AI systems show unfair preferences or make unfair decisions, often reflecting biases present in their training data. A critical risk to monitor in HR applications.
Context Window
The amount of information (text, documents) an AI can "remember" or process at one time. Modern models like Gemini 2.5 have massive context windows, allowing them to analyze entire books or policy libraries at once.
Fine-tuning
Adapting a pre-trained AI model to better handle specific tasks or match your organization's unique style. Like training a new employee to follow your company's specific procedures.
Generative AI
AI that can create new content (like writing, images, or code) rather than just analyzing existing information. This is the technology behind tools like ChatGPT and Claude.
Hallucination
When an AI makes up information that sounds plausible but isn't true or factual. A primary reason why all AI output must be verified by a human expert.
Large Language Model (LLM)
An AI system trained on vast amounts of text that can understand and generate human-like writing. The core technology of generative AI.
Multimodal
An AI that can understand and process multiple types of information at once, such as text, images, audio, and data. You can give it a screenshot of a spreadsheet and ask questions about it.
Open-Source
AI models (like Meta's Llama) whose code is publicly available. They can be downloaded and run on your own private servers, offering maximum data privacy and control, but require technical expertise.
Prompt
The instructions or questions you give to the AI. The skill of writing effective prompts is a key part of using AI successfully.
RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)
A system that lets an AI "look up" information from a specific set of documents you provide (like your Employee Handbook) *before* it answers a question. This makes its answers more accurate and grounded in your company's facts. This is now a common feature in enterprise AI tools.
Token
The basic units of text that AI processes. A token is roughly 3-4 characters of English text. A model's "context window" is measured in tokens (e.g., a 1 million token context window).

When evaluating vendors, look for these modern features:
